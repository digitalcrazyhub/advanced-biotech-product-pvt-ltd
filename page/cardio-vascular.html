How It Performs in Cardiac Tissue

Closure that holds — and tissue that takes over

The heart never stops moving. A patch material used inside it has to resist suture pull-through under constant cyclical strain on day one, then quietly disappear as the patient's own tissue rebuilds the wall behind it.

  1. Defect is sized and trimmed to fit

    The mesh is cut intraoperatively to match the defect margins, with sufficient overlap onto healthy tissue to distribute tension evenly along the suture line.

  2. Continuous or interrupted suture fixation

    The collagen matrix holds suture bites cleanly without fraying or tearing, supporting both continuous running closures and interrupted mattress sutures depending on surgeon preference.

  3. Scaffold bears load while cells migrate in

    In the early postoperative period, the matrix itself provides the mechanical seal across the defect, while fibroblasts and endothelial cells begin populating the structure from the wound margins.

  4. Progressive remodeling replaces the scaffold

    As native collagen deposition and endothelialization proceed, the implanted matrix is resorbed and replaced by the patient's own tissue, rather than persisting as a permanent foreign material.

Where Surgeons Use It

Cardiac and cardiovascular applications

Surgicoll-Mesh is used across a range of structural cardiac repairs where a pliable, suturable, biologically resorbable patch is preferred over a permanent synthetic material.

Septal Repair

Atrial septal defect (ASD) closure

Patch closure of secundum and other ASDs where primary suture closure would create excessive tension across the septum.

Septal Repair

Ventricular septal defect (VSD) closure

Reinforcement or patch closure of ventricular septal defects, supporting the higher-pressure environment of the left ventricle.

Pericardium

Pericardial reconstruction

Substitute or reinforcement material following pericardiectomy or pericardial resection, helping restrict adhesion formation to adjacent structures.

Chamber Repair

Atrial and ventricular wall reconstruction

Repair of chamber wall defects arising from trauma, tumor resection, or post-infarction complications such as ventricular free wall rupture.

Valve-Adjacent

Annular and valve-adjacent reconstruction

Reinforcement of tissue adjacent to valve repair sites where supplementary structural support is needed alongside annuloplasty.

Congenital

Congenital heart defect repair

Patch material for a range of congenital structural repairs in pediatric and adult congenital cardiac surgery, sized to the defect at hand.

Material Properties

Built for the demands of cardiac surgery

Four properties matter most when a patch material sits inside a contracting chamber, under direct blood contact, for the life of the repair.

Suture retention

Holds suture bites under tension without tearing, supporting both running and interrupted closure techniques along the defect margin.

Conformability

Drapes and conforms to irregular or curved defect geometry across septal and chamber wall anatomy without buckling.

Hemostatic surface

Supports early hemostasis at the suture line during a phase when the repair is in direct contact with circulating blood.

Controlled resorption

Degrades in step with native tissue remodeling, avoiding a permanent synthetic implant in load-bearing cardiac tissue.

Material Selection

Collagen patch vs. synthetic patch materials

Surgeons weigh patch choice against the demands of the specific repair. Here is how a resorbable collagen matrix compares with permanent synthetic options such as PTFE or Dacron patches.

Clinical Considerations

What to weigh before use

As with any structural cardiac repair material, patch selection should be individualized to the defect, the hemodynamic environment, and the patient.

Defect size and location

Larger defects and higher-pressure chambers (left atrium, ventricles) place greater mechanical demand on the patch and suture line than smaller, lower-pressure septal defects.

Hemodynamic environment

Pressure and flow conditions at the repair site should inform patch selection and the surgical closure technique used to secure it.

Patient growth considerations

In pediatric and congenital repair, the eventual replacement of a resorbable scaffold by native, growth-capable tissue is a relevant factor in material choice.

Surgeon technique and preference

As with any patch material, handling characteristics, suture technique, and intraoperative trimming should follow standard cardiac surgical practice and surgeon judgment.

Surgical Note
